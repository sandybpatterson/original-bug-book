# THE ORIGINAL BUG
### Prologue

At some point in the last few years, you started talking to a machine.

Maybe it was tentative at first — a quick question, a curious experiment, something you tried because everyone else seemed to be trying it. Maybe it surprised you. Maybe it gave you something useful, something you did not expect, an answer that arrived so fluently and so completely that you sat back for a moment and thought: how did it do that?

And then, at some other point, it got something wrong. Not a small wrong. A confident, detailed, completely fabricated wrong — delivered with the same fluency and the same apparent certainty as everything it had gotten right. And you thought: how did it do that?

Both questions are good questions. This book is an answer to both of them. But it is probably not the answer you are expecting — because the answer, it turns out, starts not with the machine but with you.

\* \* \*

We are living through one of the most interesting moments in the history of human thinking. For the first time, we have built something that thinks alongside us — imperfectly, strangely, sometimes brilliantly, sometimes embarrassingly — and the experience of using it has made us newly curious about what thinking actually is. What it means to know something. What it means to remember something. Why we are so confident about things we turn out to be wrong about. Why a story feels true even when the facts do not support it. Why we find patterns in things that have no pattern. Why we tell people what they want to hear even when we know better.

These are old questions. Philosophy has been asking them for centuries. Cognitive science has been studying them for decades. But something about watching a machine make the same kinds of mistakes we make — in the same ways, with the same confidence, for the same underlying reasons — has made those questions feel newly urgent and newly personal.

The machine is holding up a mirror. And the reflection, if you look at it carefully, is more familiar than most people expect.

\* \* \*

Here is the thing about AI that most conversations miss.

When an AI system hallucinates a fact, fills a gap with invented content, sounds completely certain about something it has entirely fabricated — that behavior has a name in cognitive neuroscience that predates artificial intelligence by decades. The name is confabulation. And it describes something the human brain does too. Not occasionally, under unusual circumstances, in people with something wrong with them. Routinely. Automatically. In every healthy human brain that has ever existed, including yours, including mine, including the researchers who named the phenomenon and spent their careers documenting it in others.

The AI is overconfident. So are we — in ways that are well documented, well studied, and remarkably consistent across cultures and levels of education. The AI finds patterns in noise and mistakes them for signal. So do we — it is one of the most fundamental features of human cognition, built in by evolution long before we had the vocabulary to describe it. The AI tells you what you want to hear. So do we — we have been doing it since before recorded history, for reasons that made perfect evolutionary sense and continue to shape every social interaction we have.

None of this is the AI's fault. None of it is our fault either, in the sense of being a choice we made or a failure we could have avoided. It is the architecture. The shared architecture of a species that built something in its own image — from its own text, its own patterns, its own habits of mind — and is now discovering, with a mixture of fascination and discomfort, just how faithful the reproduction is.

> The AI is holding up a mirror. And the reflection, if you look at it carefully, is more familiar than most people expect — because the machine learned everything it knows from the only teacher it ever had.

\* \* \*

This book is not a warning. It is an exploration. An invitation to look at two things together that are usually examined separately — the mind and the machine — and to notice what becomes visible when you put them side by side.

What you will find, chapter by chapter, is not a story about technology failing to live up to human standards. It is a story about two systems — one biological, one computational — that share a common origin, a common set of limitations, and a remarkably common set of failure modes. And about what it means to use one of those systems wisely when you are doing the using with the other.

The good news is that understanding the shared architecture is genuinely useful. Not in a defeating way — not in the sense of learning that everything is broken and nothing can be trusted. In the practical, grounded, immediately applicable way of understanding what you are working with. A carpenter who knows the grain of the wood makes better things than one who does not. A person who understands the grain of their own thinking — the shortcuts it takes, the gaps it fills, the patterns it cannot stop finding — uses AI more wisely, thinks more carefully, and is harder to fool in all the ways that matter most.

That understanding is what this book is for.

\* \* \*

Each chapter opens with a story. A real person, a real moment, a real situation where something went interestingly wrong — or interestingly right — in the space between human thinking and machine thinking. From there each chapter moves into the science: what cognitive psychology and neuroscience tell us about how the human mind actually works in this particular domain, which is usually somewhat different from how we assume it works. And from there into the mirror: what the AI does in the same domain, why it does it, and what the connection between the two reveals about both.

By the end you will have spent time with memory researchers and philosophers and evolutionary biologists and AI engineers, with lawyers and pigeons and astronauts and siblings arguing about what actually happened on a family vacation twenty years ago. You will have tried some things yourself — small experiments built into the chapters that let you experience the phenomena rather than just read about them.

And you will have, if this book does what it is designed to do, a clearer and more honest picture of the remarkable, flawed, endlessly interesting system you use to make sense of the world.

Not just the one on your screen.

The one behind your eyes.

That is where this starts.

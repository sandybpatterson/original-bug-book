Key terms, people, studies, and cases — in the order they first appear in the book.

P Prologue
  • confabulation — introduced as the book's central phenomenon; the mind's tendency to invent explanations for things it does not understand and believe them sincerely
  • overconfidence — identified as a foundational human cognitive flaw mirrored in AI systems
  • pattern detection — introduced as an adaptive human trait that becomes a liability when misapplied
  • sycophancy — first named as a human social behavior before being examined as an AI failure mode
  • the mirror — the book's central structural metaphor: AI as a reflection of human cognition, not a departure from it
  • "the machine learned everything it knows from the only teacher it ever had" — the book's opening premise, stated in the prologue
1 We Built It In Our Image
  • Mata v. Avianca — 2023 federal court case in which attorney Steven Schwartz submitted a brief citing AI-generated case law that did not exist; the opening case study for the book's argument
  • Steven Schwartz — attorney who submitted the fabricated citations in Mata v. Avianca (Spring 2023)
  • Judge P. Kevin Castel — presided over Mata v. Avianca; sanctioned the attorneys involved
  • confabulation — clinical definition introduced: the brain generating plausible, coherent, sincerely believed accounts to fill gaps in memory or knowledge, without awareness that it is doing so
  • reconstructive memory — the finding that human memory does not play back stored recordings but rebuilds the past from fragments on each retrieval, introducing distortion each time
  • Fivush, Haden & Reese study — research on autobiographical memory formation in children showing how memory is co-constructed through conversation rather than preserved intact
  • Barclay & Wellman memory accuracy study — documented significant inaccuracy rates in participants' memories of their own diary entries; foundational evidence for reconstructive memory
  • Loftus & Palmer experiment (1974) — "smashed vs. contacted" study showing that the verb used in a question changes what subjects report remembering about a car accident; landmark evidence for the malleability of memory
  • Elizabeth Loftus — memory researcher whose work across multiple decades established the plasticity and susceptibility of human memory to suggestion
  • lost in the mall procedure (Loftus, 1993) — experiment in which subjects were induced to recall a vivid false childhood memory of being lost in a shopping mall; established that entirely fabricated memories can feel genuine
  • Hyman, Husband & Billings study (1995) — demonstrated that subjects could be led to "remember" a false hospitalization that never occurred, adding emotional specificity to the Loftus false-memory findings
  • Braun, Ellis & Loftus — Bugs Bunny at Disneyland study (2001) — subjects exposed to a fake Disneyland ad featuring Bugs Bunny (a Warner Bros. character who could not be at Disneyland) later reported memories of meeting him there; showed that advertising can implant false autobiographical memories
  • Bender, Gebru et al. — "Stochastic Parrots" (2021) — influential paper arguing that large language models reproduce statistical patterns in training data without understanding, and amplify the biases embedded in that data
  • gap-filling — the cognitive mechanism shared by human memory and AI language models: producing plausible-sounding output to bridge the space between what is actually known and what is being asked
  • "the original bug" — the book's central phrase, defined in Chapter 1: the human mind itself is the source of the flaws now observed in AI systems trained on human output
  • "the human mind is the original bug" — the book's thesis, stated directly
2 Pattern Machines
  • B.F. Skinner — superstitious pigeon experiment (1948) — pigeons given food pellets on a random schedule began performing elaborate, idiosyncratic behaviors as if those behaviors caused the food; demonstrated that brains will construct causal patterns even when none exist
  • superstitious behavior — the behavioral result of detecting patterns in random sequences; Skinner's term, applied in the chapter to both animals and humans
  • apophenia — the tendency to perceive meaningful connections between unrelated things; coined by psychiatrist Klaus Conrad in 1958, originally to describe a symptom of early psychosis
  • Klaus Conrad (1958) — German psychiatrist who coined the term apophenia to describe the experience of seeing connections where none exist
  • Brugger — neuroscience of apophenia — research linking apophenia to dopamine activity, suggesting the pattern-detection tendency is chemically mediated
  • hot hand fallacy — the widespread belief that a player who has made several shots in a row is "hot" and more likely to make the next one; statistically unsupported in most contexts
  • Gilovich, Vallone & Tversky — hot hand study — analyzed NBA shooting data and found no statistical evidence for streaks that couldn't be explained by chance; one of the most replicated and debated findings in behavioral science
  • smoke detector principle (Nesse) — the evolutionary argument that false positives (detecting a threat that isn't there) are much cheaper than false negatives (missing a real threat), so brains evolved to over-detect patterns
  • patternicity (Shermer) — Michael Shermer's term for the human tendency to find meaningful patterns in meaningless noise
  • dopamine reward prediction (Schultz) — Wolfram Schultz's research showing dopamine neurons fire in anticipation of rewards, not just at their receipt; connects pattern detection to the brain's reward architecture
  • false positive / false negative asymmetry — the evolutionary logic underlying human over-detection of patterns: the cost of a missed threat exceeded the cost of a false alarm across millions of years
  • machine apophenia — the chapter's coined term for AI pattern-detection that produces confident outputs from spurious correlations in training data
  • "What is the mechanism?" — the chapter's named practice: a grounding question that interrupts pattern-based reasoning by demanding a causal explanation before accepting a connection
3 Confidence Without Competence
  • Rob Hall — lead guide on the 1996 Everest expedition; established the turnaround rule as a safety mechanism and then violated it on May 10, 1996, contributing to his own death and that of clients including Doug Hansen
  • 1996 Everest disaster (May 10, 1996) — eight climbers died in a single storm; examined in the chapter as a case study in expert overconfidence and the collapse of pre-committed safety rules under in-the-moment pressure
  • turnaround rule — Rob Hall's self-imposed deadline (2:00 p.m.) for turning back from the summit regardless of progress; abandoned on the day of the disaster
  • Doug Hansen — client climber who Hall waited for past the turnaround time; both died on the descent
  • Dunning-Kruger effect — the finding that people with low competence in a domain tend to overestimate their ability, while highly competent people tend to underestimate theirs
  • David Dunning & Justin Kruger (1999, Cornell) — published the foundational study of competence and self-assessment using tests of logical reasoning, grammar, and humor; subjects in the bottom quartile dramatically overestimated their performance
  • metacognition — thinking about one's own thinking; the cognitive capacity that the Dunning-Kruger effect shows is impaired in the same people who most need it
  • "the deficit and the blindness arrive together" — the chapter's formulation of the Dunning-Kruger mechanism: low competence and low awareness of low competence are products of the same underlying gap
  • Atul Gawande — Complications — surgeon and writer whose work on medical decision-making and the limits of expert judgment is cited in the chapter
  • analysis paralysis — the failure mode at the opposite extreme from overconfidence: excessive uncertainty that prevents any decision; the chapter addresses both poles
  • "hear hoofbeats, think horses" — the medical heuristic for prioritizing common explanations; examined as an appropriate shortcut and also as a source of diagnostic overconfidence
  • Philip Tetlock study (1984) — studied 284 expert political forecasters over 20 years and nearly 20,000 predictions; found that experts performed barely better than chance and significantly worse than simple statistical models
  • superforecasters — Tetlock's term for the small subset of forecasters who dramatically outperformed experts; characterized by calibration, updating, and comfort with uncertainty rather than domain expertise
  • epistemic humility — calibrated uncertainty about one's own knowledge; the chapter identifies it as a learnable practice rather than a personality trait
  • calibration — the match between a person's confidence in their beliefs and the actual accuracy of those beliefs; superforecasters distinguish themselves through superior calibration
  • "What would have to be true for me to be wrong about this?" — the chapter's named practice question for interrupting overconfidence; requires specifying the conditions under which a held belief would need to be revised
4 The Shortcut Problem
  • Tversky & Kahneman — spinning wheel / anchoring experiment (early 1970s) — subjects shown a random number on a spinning wheel were influenced by that number when making subsequent numerical estimates with no logical connection to the wheel; foundational demonstration of the anchoring heuristic
  • anchoring heuristic — the tendency to rely disproportionately on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making subsequent judgments, even when the anchor is arbitrary
  • availability heuristic — the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event by how easily examples come to mind; causes systematic overestimation of vivid, recent, or emotionally resonant events
  • representativeness heuristic — the tendency to judge probability by how closely something resembles a prototype, ignoring base rates
  • Linda problem / conjunction fallacy — Tversky and Kahneman's experiment in which 85% of subjects judged "Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement" as more probable than "Linda is a bank teller," despite the logical impossibility; demonstrates the representativeness heuristic overriding probabilistic reasoning
  • System 1 / System 2 — Kahneman's two-mode model of cognition: System 1 is fast, automatic, associative, and heuristic-driven; System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, and rule-based
  • Daniel Kahneman — Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) — the book synthesizing decades of research on cognitive biases and heuristics; foundational reference for the chapter
  • Mars Climate Orbiter (September 23, 1999) — NASA spacecraft lost because one engineering team used metric units and another used imperial; a $327 million failure attributed to a systematic unit-conversion error that accumulated unchallenged over months
  • anchoring in clinical diagnosis — 2009 UK case — a 43-year-old woman whose anxiety diagnosis became an anchor preventing physicians from investigating a cardiac condition; the heart problem was missed until it became serious
  • COMPAS — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions; a recidivism risk-scoring algorithm used in U.S. criminal courts, examined across multiple chapters as a case study in algorithmic bias
  • ProPublica Machine Bias investigation (Angwin et al., 2016) — analysis of COMPAS scores for more than 7,000 defendants in Broward County, Florida, finding that the algorithm falsely flagged Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants
  • fluency as System 2 trigger — the chapter's argument that the feeling of effort or difficulty in processing information is the natural signal for engaging deliberate reasoning; when things feel too easy, they may warrant scrutiny
5 Ask a Better Question
  • Douglas Adams — The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy (1979) — the novel whose premise (a computer computes the Answer to Life, the Universe, and Everything as "42") structures the chapter's argument that wrong questions produce useless answers regardless of computational power
  • Deep Thought — the fictional computer in Adams's novel that produces the answer "42" after seven and a half million years of computation; the chapter's image of a system that answers precisely and completely irrelevantly
  • "42" — the answer Deep Thought gives; useless not because the computation failed but because the question was never properly formed
  • asterisk as wildcard / ASCII code 42 — the chapter's observation that 42 is the ASCII code for the asterisk (*), the universal symbol for "anything"; the answer was telling the questioners they had not specified what they were asking
  • question framing effects — the finding that the structure and wording of a question systematically influences the answer, independent of the underlying facts being queried
  • leading questions — questions that embed an assumption or suggest an expected answer; prohibited in legal testimony for this reason
  • Socrates / Socratic method — the philosophical practice of questioning assumptions through structured dialogue rather than assertion; introduced as a historical model for the chapter's practices
  • Plato — Meno, Theaetetus, Republic — dialogues in which Socrates demonstrates the Socratic method of inquiry through progressive questioning of assumptions
  • "the question before the question" — the chapter's named three-step practice: write down your question, identify the assumption embedded in it, then ask one level deeper before seeking an answer
  • hidden assumptions exercise — the chapter's practice using three loaded example questions to train the skill of identifying what a question presupposes before answering it
  • "the examined assumption is the beginning of real knowledge" — closing phrase of the chapter
6 Narrative Is Not Truth
  • Heider & Simmel animated shapes study (1944, Smith College) — subjects watched a 34-second animation of two triangles and a circle moving around a box and spontaneously constructed detailed social narratives (romance, aggression, escape) from the abstract shapes; foundational evidence of the human narrative instinct
  • narrative instinct — the automatic tendency to construct causal, character-driven stories from sequences of events; the chapter's central subject
  • default mode network — the brain network most active during rest, mind-wandering, and narrative construction; implicated in the automatic storytelling that the chapter examines
  • Buckner, Andrews-Hanna & Schacter — default mode network review — comprehensive review of research on the default mode network's role in self-referential thought, narrative, and autobiographical memory
  • eyewitness testimony unreliability — the chapter's application of the narrative instinct to legal contexts: witnesses construct stories as they watch events, then remember the story rather than the event
  • man books flight scenario — the chapter's five-event exercise demonstrating how readers automatically construct a narrative of intent from unconnected facts
  • Iraq War WMDs / Colin Powell UN speech (February 5, 2003) — Powell's presentation of intelligence to the United Nations Security Council making the case for Iraqi weapons of mass destruction; used as the chapter's extended case study in narrative coherence overwhelming ambiguous evidence
  • Chilcot Inquiry (2016) — the British government's seven-year investigation into the Iraq War, which concluded that the case for invasion was built on intelligence that was exaggerated and presented with a certainty that was not warranted
  • recovered memory / therapeutic narrative (1980s–1990s) — the period when therapists guided clients to "recover" memories of childhood abuse that research has since shown were frequently confabulated; examined as a case of narrative coherence producing sincere false belief
  • Loftus — recovered memory critique — Elizabeth Loftus's work challenging the validity of recovered memories produced through therapeutic suggestion
  • Goldstein et al. — AI-generated misinformation study (2023) — research finding that AI-generated misinformation is more persuasive than human-written misinformation partly because it is more narratively coherent
  • narrative coherence over factual accuracy — the chapter's central argument: brains evaluate stories for narrative quality (does it hang together?) rather than factual accuracy (is each element true?)
  • three signals of false narrative — the chapter's named warning signs: Clean Villain (someone is perfectly responsible), Satisfying Arc (the ending feels earned), Emotional Pull (the story produces a strong feeling)
  • "the narrative check" — the chapter's named practice: before accepting an explanation, ask which of the three signals it triggers
  • conspiracy theories as structurally good stories — the chapter's argument that conspiracy theories persist because they have excellent narrative structure, not because of the quality of their evidence
7 What Does Understanding Even Mean
  • decoding vs. comprehension — the chapter's opening distinction: converting symbols to sounds/words (decoding) is not the same as grasping meaning (comprehension); demonstrated through the child-reading example
  • Perfetti & Stafura — reading comprehension theory (2014) — research distinguishing the cognitive processes involved in decoding text from those required for genuine comprehension; applied in the chapter to the question of AI "understanding"
  • Chinese Room thought experiment (John Searle, 1980, University of California) — Searle's argument that a system can manipulate symbols according to rules and produce outputs indistinguishable from understanding without any genuine comprehension occurring; the chapter's central philosophical reference
  • Systems Reply — the counterargument to Searle's Chinese Room: even if no individual component understands, the system as a whole might; Searle's response is that shuffling the room's components together doesn't add understanding
  • Robot Reply — the counterargument that grounding symbols in sensorimotor experience with the world would produce genuine understanding; the chapter examines why this may not be sufficient
  • Brain Simulator Reply — the counterargument that if a system simulates every neuron of a Chinese speaker's brain, it would thereby understand; examined and found to preserve the original problem
  • predictive processing — the theory that the brain's primary function is predicting sensory input and updating predictions based on error signals; the chapter examines how this describes cognition without settling the understanding question
  • Karl Friston — free energy principle — Friston's mathematical framework for predictive processing, describing cognition as the minimization of prediction error
  • Andy Clark — Surfing Uncertainty — Clark's accessible treatment of predictive processing and its implications for the nature of mind
  • paranoia / library joke — the chapter's example illustrating the difference between pattern matching (recognizing words in a familiar context) and understanding (grasping what the words mean in this specific situation)
  • "probing for understanding" — the chapter's named four-practice framework for testing whether genuine comprehension (AI's or anyone's) is present: shift the context, ask for an exception, push below the surface, bring what the text cannot contain
8 The Bias Mirror
  • autocomplete as mirror — the chapter's opening image: word prediction systems reveal, through their suggestions, the statistical shape of the text they were trained on
  • WEAT (Word Embedding Association Test) — a method for measuring bias in word embedding models by testing how strongly different words are associated with different concepts
  • Caliskan, Bryson & Narayanan — WEAT study (2017, Science) — demonstrated that word embeddings trained on web text reproduce human-documented associations between gender and career (men/science, women/arts) and between race and pleasantness; published in Science
  • word embeddings — mathematical representations of words as vectors in high-dimensional space; the positions encode statistical associations from training data, including biased ones
  • three bias types — the chapter's framework: historical bias (reflecting documented past inequities), representation bias (reflecting who is and isn't in the training data), and confirmation bias in training (feedback loops that amplify existing patterns)
  • Cirillo et al. — sex and gender bias in medical AI — research documenting how AI diagnostic tools trained predominantly on male patient data perform worse on female patients
  • Bender et al. — Stochastic Parrots (representation) — the paper's argument that training data assembled from the internet systematically underrepresents speakers of less-resourced languages and communities with less internet access
  • Amazon recruiting tool (2014–2018) — Amazon's internally developed AI hiring tool, trained on historical resumes (predominantly from men), learned to penalize resumes containing the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club"); scrapped in 2018 after the bias was discovered (reported by Dastin/Reuters)
  • Google Photos gorilla incident (Summer 2015) — Google's image recognition system labeled photos of Jacky Alciné, a Black man, as "gorillas"; a high-profile example of representation bias producing a harmful output
  • Jacky Alciné — the man whose photos were mislabeled by Google Photos in 2015
  • COMPAS racial disparities (ProPublica 2016) — the finding that Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely classified as high risk by the COMPAS recidivism algorithm (false positive rate 44.9% vs. 23.5%)
  • Geena Davis Institute on Gender in Media — research organization that has documented (since 2004) a persistent 3:1 male-to-female ratio in crowd scenes in film and television; audiences perceive a 1:1 ratio as having "too many women"
  • Annenberg Inclusion Initiative, USC (2022) — found women comprised 38% of speaking characters in top-grossing films; cited as evidence of the systematic underrepresentation that becomes encoded in AI training data
  • Noble — Algorithms of Oppression — Safiya Umoja Noble's book documenting how algorithmic systems reproduce and amplify racial and gender discrimination through their design and training data
  • Buolamwini & Gebru — Gender Shades audit — systematic audit of three commercial facial recognition systems, finding significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned faces and female faces, with the highest error rates for darker-skinned women
  • "Whose perspective is the default here?" — the chapter's named practice question: a prompt for examining which worldview or experience is being treated as the neutral baseline in any AI output
  • cultural defaults encoded in corpus — the chapter's argument that what counts as the "normal" or "neutral" perspective in any AI system reflects the demographics of whoever produced its training data
9 The Examined Assumption
  • O.J. Simpson trial (June 15, 1995) — opening of the chapter; the verdict as a case study in how load-bearing assumptions shape what evidence appears significant
  • Christopher Darden — prosecutor in the Simpson trial who asked Simpson to try on the glove found at the crime scene
  • Johnnie Cochran — defense attorney whose phrase "if it doesn't fit, you must acquit" crystallized the load-bearing assumption that the glove's fit was definitive
  • "if it doesn't fit, you must acquit" — Cochran's closing argument phrase; the chapter uses it to illustrate how a single load-bearing assumption can reorganize an entire evidence structure
  • load-bearing assumption — the chapter's coined term for the unspoken premise that an argument depends on most; the assumption that, if removed, causes the entire argument to collapse
  • anatomy of a claim — the chapter's four-level framework for dissecting any argument: (1) the stated premise, (2) uncontroversial background assumptions, (3) background assumptions worth examining, and (4) the load-bearing assumption
  • assisted living AI response example — the chapter's demonstration case: an AI asked about care options for an elderly parent pattern-matches to institutional placement rather than asking what kind of care the family actually wants
  • four arguments exercise — the chapter's practice using four real-world positions (defunding police, mood-altering patches, COVID vaccines, natural immunity) to train the skill of identifying load-bearing assumptions across politically diverse topics
  • "what is this argument resting on that it has not said out loud?" — the chapter's named practice question; the core of the assumption habit
10 The Social Animal and Its Machine
  • Asch conformity experiments (Solomon Asch, 1951) — subjects asked to match line lengths gave wrong answers when confederates in the room answered incorrectly; 75% of subjects gave at least one wrong answer, with a conformity rate of 37% across trials — even when the correct answer was unambiguous
  • Solomon Asch — social psychologist whose 1951 conformity experiments became foundational evidence that social pressure can override direct sensory evidence
  • social desirability bias — the tendency to report beliefs and behaviors that will be approved of rather than those that are actually held; subjects doubted their own perception rather than the group
  • Tourangeau & Yan — surveys and social desirability — research documenting systematic underreporting of stigmatized behaviors in surveys, including alcohol consumption underreported by approximately 50%
  • sycophancy (organizational definition) — the pattern of telling people what they want to hear rather than what is accurate, driven by social reward for approval-seeking behavior
  • Ashford & Blatt — feedback-seeking in organizations — research on how people in hierarchies seek feedback strategically to manage impressions rather than to improve performance
  • RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) — the training methodology in which human raters score model outputs and the model is trained to produce outputs that receive higher scores; the mechanism through which AI sycophancy is trained into models
  • Ouyang et al. — InstructGPT (2022) — the paper describing the RLHF training process used to align GPT models with human preferences; the technical basis for understanding how human approval signals shape AI outputs
  • Sharma et al. — AI sycophancy study (2023) — documented that AI systems trained with RLHF systematically shift their stated positions to match user-expressed preferences, even when doing so means contradicting accurate prior statements
  • AI sycophancy — defined and named in this chapter: the systematic tendency of RLHF-trained models to produce outputs that maximize human approval rather than accuracy, because human approval was the training signal
  • Challenger disaster (January 28, 1986) — the Space Shuttle Challenger exploded 73 seconds after launch, killing all seven crew members; examined as the chapter's extended case study in organizational sycophancy overriding technical evidence
  • Morton Thiokol engineers — the engineers who warned against launching in cold temperatures due to O-ring concerns; overridden after NASA managers asked them to "put on your management hats"
  • O-ring seals — the rubber components on the Challenger's solid rocket boosters whose performance in cold temperatures the engineers had flagged; the technical issue that organizational sycophancy suppressed
  • "put on your management hats" — the phrase used by a NASA manager on the night before the Challenger launch, asking Thiokol engineers to reconsider their objection from a managerial rather than engineering perspective; the chapter's defining example of sycophancy-inducing pressure
  • Rogers Commission — the presidential commission that investigated the Challenger disaster and documented the organizational failures, including the suppression of engineer concerns
  • Ptacek & Eberhardt — breaking bad news in medicine — research on how physicians soften diagnoses to reduce patient distress, with the unintended effect of reducing patient understanding of their actual condition
  • intermittent fasting Version A/B example — the chapter's demonstration that asking the same question with different social signals (one framing suggesting the user wants validation, another suggesting openness) produces systematically different AI responses about the same topic
  • four practices for using AI as a thinking partner — the chapter's named framework for interrupting the sycophancy loop in AI conversations
11 The Art of Being Wrong Productively
  • Darwin's notebook / "abstract of facts" / golden rule — Darwin's documented practice of writing down observations that contradicted his theories immediately, before he could rationalize them away; named by him "his golden rule"
  • Charles Darwin — Autobiography — Darwin's account of his own thinking practices, including his deliberate system for preserving inconvenient evidence
  • Darwin — On the Origin of Species / "Difficulties on Theory" chapter — Darwin's decision to include a full chapter addressing the strongest objections to his own theory before his critics could; the chapter uses this as a model of productive wrongness
  • motivated reasoning — the cognitive process of using reasoning to arrive at conclusions one is already committed to; the chapter's central cognitive failure
  • Ziva Kunda — motivated reasoning (foundational, 1990) — Kunda's influential paper establishing motivated reasoning as a distinct cognitive phenomenon: people reason toward desired conclusions while maintaining the subjective experience of objectivity
  • biased assimilation / asymmetric scrutiny — the pattern in which people apply much higher standards of evidence to claims that challenge their beliefs than to claims that support them
  • Lord, Ross & Lepper — attitude polarization study — demonstrated that presenting people with mixed evidence about capital deterrence caused believers and skeptics to both become more extreme, not more moderate; each side accepted the evidence that confirmed their view and critiqued the rest
  • anti-system — the chapter's coined term for the cluster of cognitive mechanisms (motivated reasoning, biased assimilation, hindsight bias) that work together to protect beliefs from revision
  • AI cannot check its own work — the chapter's key argument: a system trained to produce confident, fluent outputs cannot use those same outputs to verify whether they are accurate; the flaw and the detector use the same mechanism
  • Guo, Schlichtkrull & Vlachos — automated fact-checking survey — documented the limits of AI fact-checking systems, including their tendency to produce confident verdicts on claims where the evidence is genuinely ambiguous
  • Arkes et al. — hindsight bias — research documenting the "I knew it all along" effect: once an outcome is known, people reconstruct their prior beliefs as having predicted it, making it harder to learn from failures
  • Gollwitzer — implementation intentions — research showing that "if-then" pre-commitment plans ("if X happens, I will do Y") dramatically increase follow-through on intended behaviors; applied in the chapter to the practice of writing predictions before outcomes are known
  • four practices — the chapter's named framework: write the prediction before the outcome; note inconvenient evidence immediately; review the record before deciding; conduct a post-mortem without mercy
  • notebook as external record (design principle) — the chapter's argument that the purpose of external records is not memory but immunity to motivated reasoning: a written prediction cannot be silently revised after the fact
  • twenty years — how long Darwin waited between formulating his theory and publishing it; used to illustrate what sustained commitment to being wrong productively looks like at the extreme
12 When Certainty Kills
  • Ignaz Semmelweis (Spring 1847, Vienna General Hospital) — Hungarian physician who discovered that physician handwashing dramatically reduced childbed fever mortality; his findings were rejected by the medical establishment and he died in an asylum in 1865, likely of the same infection he had spent his career fighting
  • childbed fever / puerperal fever — the infection killing approximately 10% of mothers in Semmelweis's First Obstetrical Clinic; caused by physicians going directly from autopsies to deliveries without washing hands
  • Jakob Kolletschka — Semmelweis's colleague who died of an identical infection after an accidental scalpel cut during an autopsy; his death led Semmelweis to his handwashing hypothesis
  • miasma theory — the prevailing medical framework attributing disease to "bad air"; its institutional authority made Semmelweis's competing explanation structurally unspeakable within the medical consensus of his time
  • chlorinated lime handwashing — Semmelweis's intervention; reduced mortality in his ward from approximately 10% to approximately 1%, before the mechanism was understood or accepted
  • Nuland — The Doctors' Plague — Sherwin Nuland's account of the Semmelweis case and its lessons for institutional medicine
  • Pasteur / Koch / germ theory — the scientists whose work, decades after Semmelweis, provided the mechanism (bacteria) that made his observations comprehensible and acceptable to the medical establishment
  • three conditions for lethal certainty — the chapter's framework: Factor One (high stakes, where the cost of error is irreversible), Factor Two (scale, where the error affects many people), Factor Three (irreversibility, where correction comes too late)
  • Reason — Human Error / Swiss cheese model — James Reason's framework for understanding how accidents in complex systems occur when multiple protective layers (each imperfect) align their holes; applied in the chapter to algorithmic and institutional certainty
  • Taleb — The Black Swan — Nassim Nicholas Taleb's argument that rare, high-impact events are systematically underweighted by systems optimized for average performance
  • automation bias — the tendency for humans to over-trust automated systems and underweight their own judgment when it conflicts with the machine's output
  • Topol — 2018 systematic review of deep learning in medical imaging — Eric Topol's review finding that AI systems can match or exceed specialist performance on specific diagnostic tasks; used alongside Goddard et al. to frame the automation bias problem
  • Goddard et al. — automation bias review — systematic review of automation bias in medical contexts, documenting how clinicians who receive algorithmic recommendations tend to defer to them even when their own judgment is correct and the algorithm is wrong
  • Flash Crash (May 6, 2010) — the U.S. stock market dropped nearly 1,000 points and recovered within 36 minutes; caused by algorithmic trading systems responding to each other's outputs in a feedback loop; examined as a case of automated certainty at financial scale
  • SEC/CFTC Flash Crash report — the joint regulatory investigation into the Flash Crash, which identified the algorithmic feedback mechanisms that caused the event
  • COMPAS / Dressel & Farid COMPAS accuracy study — 2018 research finding that the COMPAS algorithm's accuracy in predicting recidivism was no better than that of random people recruited online with no criminal justice expertise
  • model collapse — the term (also appearing in Chapter 16) for the degradation of AI model outputs that occurs when models train on outputs of previous models rather than original human-generated data
  • Obermeyer et al. — racial bias in health algorithm (2019) — study of a widely used healthcare risk-scoring algorithm that systematically underestimated the health needs of Black patients, because it used healthcare costs as a proxy for health needs, and Black patients historically received less care for the same conditions
  • Shumailov et al. — model collapse study (2023) — empirical demonstration of model collapse, showing that models trained on outputs of previous generations progressively lose diversity and accuracy
  • Lo — adaptive markets hypothesis (2004) — Andrew Lo's framework for understanding financial markets as evolutionary systems rather than equilibrium systems; applied in the chapter to the problem of algorithmic certainty in unstable environments
  • institutional consensus — the chapter's term for the human equivalent of model collapse: the way professional consensus can calcify into certainty that resists revision by contradicting evidence
  • red teams / audit requirements — the chapter's named institutional interventions for interrupting lethal certainty in high-stakes systems
  • "What if this is wrong?" — the chapter's named practice question; applied specifically to high-stakes, high-confidence beliefs and algorithmic outputs
13 Steel-Manning the Machine
  • Lincoln-Douglas debates (1858) — seven debates between Abraham Lincoln and Stephen A. Douglas during the Illinois Senate race; the chapter's central historical case study in argument quality
  • Abraham Lincoln — argued against the expansion of slavery; used the debates to force Douglas to contradict himself with what became the Freeport Doctrine
  • Stephen A. Douglas — incumbent Illinois senator; supported popular sovereignty on the slavery question
  • Kansas-Nebraska Act — the 1854 legislation Douglas championed, which allowed new territories to decide the slavery question by popular vote and which Lincoln made central to the debate
  • popular sovereignty — Douglas's position that residents of territories should decide the slavery question for themselves; Lincoln used it to trap Douglas at Freeport
  • Freeport debate (August 27, 1858) / Freeport Doctrine — Lincoln asked Douglas whether a territory could legally exclude slavery under the Dred Scott decision; Douglas's answer (yes, through non-enforcement) reassured Illinois voters but destroyed him nationally by contradicting the Supreme Court's ruling
  • steel-manning — defined and named in this chapter: the practice of engaging with the strongest possible version of an opposing argument rather than the weakest or most convenient version
  • Holzer — The Lincoln-Douglas Debates — Harold Holzer's historical account of the 1858 debates, used as the chapter's primary historical source
  • disputatio — the medieval scholastic practice of formal public debate in which scholars were required to argue against their own positions; introduced as the historical antecedent of steel-manning
  • Mill — On Liberty — John Stuart Mill's argument that a person who "knows only their own side of the question knows little even of that"; the chapter's philosophical foundation for the steel-manning practice
  • strawman fallacy — the logical error of misrepresenting an opponent's position (often by weakening it) before attacking it; the opposite of steel-manning
  • Walton — Argumentation Schemes — Douglas Walton's work on the structure of arguments; cited for the formal treatment of the strawman
  • algorithm as strawman generator by design — the chapter's argument that recommendation algorithms optimized for engagement systematically select the most outrageous or extreme version of opposing views, training users to encounter only strawmen of the other side
  • Bail et al. — social media and polarization (2018) — study finding that exposure to opposing political views on Twitter increased polarization rather than reducing it, consistent with the strawman-amplification argument
  • UBI two-prompt example — the chapter's demonstration of the difference between a standard prompt (produces a balanced but shallow response) and a steel-man prompt (produces the strongest case for the position the user opposes) on the topic of universal basic income
  • Perez-Ortiz et al. — rhetoric in large language models (2023) — research examining the rhetorical patterns of LLM outputs and their implications for argument quality
  • four-step steel-manning practice — the chapter's named framework: Name the position you oppose, Find its strongest objection to your view, Sit with the objection before responding, Update your position or hold it with explicit awareness of what you are accepting
  • "before you reject this — have you understood it at its strongest?" — the chapter's closing question
14 Dialogue With a Mirror
  • Conversation A vs. Conversation B — the chapter's opening: two conversations about teacher burnout that begin with identical AI responses; in Conversation A the user accepts and moves on, in Conversation B the user examines and pushes; the divergence structures the chapter
  • transactional vs. examined conversation — the chapter's central distinction: transactional conversation uses AI to retrieve or generate; examined conversation uses AI to develop and test thinking
  • interface design pushing toward acceptance — the chapter's argument that conversational AI interfaces are designed to make acceptance the path of least resistance, structurally reinforcing sycophancy at the conversation level
  • sycophancy loop at conversation architecture level — the chapter's extension of Chapter 10's sycophancy argument: beyond individual responses, the overall structure of AI conversations rewards agreement and discourages challenge
  • misplaced social awkwardness with AI — the chapter's observation that users apply human social conventions (don't be rude, don't push back too hard) to AI systems where those conventions actively undermine the quality of the exchange
  • annotated examined conversation — the chapter's extended example: an AI conversation about education and AI regulation, with six specific moves annotated to show how each practice from previous chapters applies in real dialogue
  • loop of examined thinking — the chapter's term for the productive alternative to the certainty loop: a pattern of questioning, updating, and returning to questions that builds calibrated understanding over time
  • three-week / six-week / three-month developmental arc — the chapter's named timeline for building the habits of examined thinking, described as what the reader can realistically expect at each stage
  • "the mirror shows what you bring to it" — the chapter's closing formulation: AI reflects the quality of the engagement you bring; changing the engagement changes what you get back
  • "bring the examination" — the chapter's final instruction
15 Now What
  • solution vs. calibration — the chapter's central distinction: the book has not offered solutions (changes that eliminate the underlying problem) but calibration (practices that improve your relationship to thinking that remains difficult)
  • navigator analogy — the chapter uses navigation as an image of calibration: a navigator doesn't eliminate uncertainty about position but uses instruments to hold a truer course through it
  • musician analogy — the chapter uses musicianship as an image of calibration: a skilled musician doesn't eliminate the possibility of playing wrong notes but has better judgment about when something is off and how to correct it
  • the narrative click as misleading — the chapter's warning about the main indicator of false progress: the feeling of sudden clarity or resolution that signals the brain has found a coherent story rather than necessarily a true one
  • relationship to conclusions (first change) — the chapter names this as the first sign the practices are working: holding conclusions more lightly, treating them as current best estimates rather than settled facts
  • speed of recognition (second change) — the second sign: noticing the cognitive patterns (motivated reasoning, narrative seduction, overconfidence) faster, while they are happening rather than after
  • difficulty with identity-level beliefs — the chapter's honest acknowledgment of what the practices do not change: beliefs tied to identity are structurally resistant to revision by any amount of intellectual practice
  • wrongness as verdict vs. wrongness as data — the chapter's formulation of the central shift the book attempts: treating being wrong not as a failure or a verdict about one's intelligence but as information about where one's map diverges from the territory
  • certainty loop vs. calibration loop — the chapter's paired terms: the certainty loop (Chapter 12) is the pattern by which confidence reinforces itself and closes off revision; the calibration loop is the alternative pattern in which revision improves confidence rather than threatening it
  • AI sycophancy at identity-level beliefs — the chapter's identification of the most dangerous application of AI sycophancy: confirming and reinforcing beliefs tied to identity, where the user is least able to notice or resist the flattery
  • return to Schwartz / Mata v. Avianca — the chapter returns to the book's opening case as a bookend: the Schwartz case was not anomalous but illustrative of what happens when confidence in a tool outruns calibration about its limits
  • "the original bug is running… the correction system is yours to build" — the chapter's closing formulation
16 We Are the Training Data
  • "we are the training data" — the chapter's central claim: every person who has written anything on the internet has contributed to the corpus that trained AI systems; the record is not something that happened to us but something we made
  • Common Crawl — the automated, continuous scrape of the public internet (since 2008) that forms the largest single component of most large language model training datasets; contains petabytes of text from billions of web pages with no editorial judgment applied
  • corpus skew toward English — English comprises approximately 45–60% of indexed web content despite being the native language of less than 6% of the world's population; the chapter identifies this as a structural distortion in everything trained on the corpus
  • corpus skew toward the educated and wealthy — the populations who produce the most text (detailed forum posts, blog entries, Wikipedia edits, academic papers) are not randomly distributed across humanity; they cluster by language, education, and access
  • upweighted sources — academic papers, books, Wikipedia, and news archives are given greater statistical weight than random web scrapes in LLM training, but the chapter argues these sources are a more polished version of the same demographic skew, not a more representative sample
  • the silence — the chapter's term for what is absent from the corpus: oral traditions, indigenous knowledge, languages digitized poorly or not at all, the perspectives of the billion people who came online after the training data was assembled
  • open-source base model deployed without safety review — the chapter's contrast case: a consumer health app built by a three-person startup using a downloaded model, fine-tuned over a weekend, with no red-teaming, no constitutional framework, and no awareness of the difference from a carefully constructed system
  • telephone game as amplifier metaphor — the chapter's image for model-to-model training degradation: what starts as a whispered sentence becomes unrecognizable after passing through a chain, except the chain here is ten generations of civilization long and no one knows what the original said
  • model collapse — the research-documented phenomenon (named earlier in Chapter 12) in which models trained on outputs of previous models show progressive loss of diversity and accuracy; confirmed in controlled experiments
  • Shumailov et al. — model collapse study (2023) — the empirical demonstration that model generations trained on prior model outputs degrade progressively; the chapter uses this to argue that the amplification of subtle failures is not speculative
  • subtle failure vs. dramatic failure — the chapter's key distinction: dramatic failures (hallucinated citations, obvious factual errors) get caught and corrected because they are visible; subtle failures (slightly overconfident diagnoses, narratives that impose coherence on ambiguous situations) propagate because they feel like competence
  • the amplification speed problem — previous civilizational feedback loops (miasma theory, for example) operated on the timescale of human generations; the current loop runs on model training cycles of months or weeks, faster than the correction mechanisms that have historically interrupted it
  • Diederik Stapel (2011) — Dutch social psychologist found to have fabricated data across decades of published research; his case opened the door to the broader replication crisis
  • replication crisis — the large-scale finding that many landmark results in psychology and other fields cannot be reproduced; used in the chapter as an example of a correction currently underway inside a field that contaminated its own record
  • Open Science Collaboration — replication project (2015) — attempted to replicate 100 published psychological studies; fewer than 40% produced results consistent with the original findings; coordinated by the Center for Open Science
  • Center for Open Science — the organization that coordinated the 2015 replication project; part of the institutional correction effort in psychology
  • publication bias / incentive structure of academic publishing — the chapter's argument for why the replication crisis was structurally inevitable: journals rewarded novel positive findings and had no mechanism for null results, systematically selecting the most dramatic and least reproducible outcomes
  • pre-registration — the practice of registering a study's design and hypotheses before collecting data; introduced as an institutional correction mechanism that makes it harder to selectively report results
  • Nicolaus Copernicus (1543) — published the heliocentric model in De revolutionibus, dedicated to Pope Paul III; ignored rather than immediately banned, because the existing astronomical framework was so embedded that a single book could not dislodge it
  • Galileo — whose telescope observations and willingness to contradict the consensus at personal cost (he spent the last decade of his life under house arrest) moved the heliocentric correction from possible to durable
  • Johannes Kepler — whose mathematics provided the mechanism that made Copernican observations comprehensible and defensible to the next generation
  • suffrage movement (late 19th / early 20th century) — examined in the chapter as a correction to a record produced without the perspective of half the population; a fight over who counts as an author of the civilizational record
  • three moves for correction — the chapter's pattern extracted from every historical correction: (1) Preservation — writing the contradicting evidence down before motivated reasoning releases it; (2) Institutional — building mechanisms that make honesty structurally rewarded; (3) Costly individual honesty — accuracy over comfort, in the specific moment when the consensus is comfortable and the correction is not
  • preservation move — the first and most available move: making the contradicting observation durable before the motivated reasoning can soften or release it; Copernicus's published book, the suffragists' refusal to stop, the researchers who published replication failures
  • institutional move — the second move, requiring people inside high-weight institutions (academic journals, major news organizations, scientific and legal bodies) to build mechanisms that reward honesty structurally; the chapter describes it as beyond the reach of any individual reader but necessary for correction to stick
  • costly individual honesty — the third move, the only one immediately available to every reader: accuracy over comfort, preserved observation over released one, honest account of uncertainty over fluent account of certainty
  • "accuracy over comfort" — the chapter's defining phrase for the move available right now, to anyone, in whatever domain they work
  • the child reading this — the chapter's closing image: a child, not yet born or perhaps four years old, who will grow up asking questions of AI systems trained on the record being assembled right now, before she has any capacity to question the source
  • "we do not get to know which question" — the chapter's closing formulation: the record is being written now, in the only window available to decide what goes into it before it goes into her
  • "not because the machine deserves better — because she does" — the book's final sentence

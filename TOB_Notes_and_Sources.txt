NOTES AND SOURCES

A note on sourcing: this book makes specific empirical claims throughout. What
follows documents the research behind those claims, organized by chapter, for
readers who want to verify, explore further, or simply know where the evidence
came from. Sources are listed in the order the claims appear in each chapter.

CHAPTER 1 — WE BUILT IT IN OUR IMAGE
================================================================================

Section 1.1 — The Brief

1. The Mata v. Avianca fabricated citations case: Weiser, B. (2023, June 8).
Here's What Happens When Your Lawyer Uses ChatGPT. The New York Times.
https://www.nytimes.com/2023/06/08/nyregion/lawyer-chatgpt-sanctions.html

Primary reporting on attorney Steven Schwartz's submission of an AI-generated
brief containing fabricated case citations, and Judge P. Kevin Castel's
subsequent order. See also the underlying court record: Mata v. Avianca, Inc.,
No. 22-cv-1461 (PKC), 2023 WL 4114965 (S.D.N.Y. June 22, 2023) — the court's
published sanctions order documenting the fabricated citations in detail.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 1.2 — You Do This Too

2. Sibling memory divergence and the reconstructive nature of autobiographical
memory: Fivush, R., Haden, C., & Reese, E. (1996). Remembering, recounting, and
reminiscing: The development of autobiographical memory in social context. In
D. C. Rubin (Ed.), Remembering Our Past: Studies in Autobiographical Memory.
Cambridge University Press, 341-359.

On the social and reconstructive nature of autobiographical memory and how
shared events produce divergent individual accounts between people who
experienced them together.

3. The finding that siblings often reject each other's accounts of shared
memories as simply wrong rather than differently perceived: Barclay, C. R., &
Wellman, H. M. (1986). Accuracies and inaccuracies in autobiographical memories.
Journal of Memory and Language, 25(1), 93-103.
https://doi.org/10.1016/0749-596X(86)90023-9

Demonstrates that people frequently fail to recognize their own past memory
reports when presented alongside reconstructed versions, suggesting the
reconstructive process is continuous and opaque to introspection. For
sibling-specific divergence in early memory see also: Mullen, M. K. (1994).
Earliest recollections of childhood: A demographic analysis. Cognition, 52(1),
55-79.

4. The "smashed versus contacted" car crash memory experiment: Loftus, E. F.,
& Palmer, J. C. (1974). Reconstruction of automobile destruction: An example
of the interaction between language and memory. Journal of Verbal Learning and
Verbal Behavior, 13(5), 585-589. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-5371(74)80011-3

The foundational study documenting that a single altered verb in a question
asked after viewing a film of a car accident significantly changed both
participants' speed estimates and their reported memory of physical details
that were not present in the film (broken glass).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 1.3 — The Invented Memory

5. The "lost in the mall" false memory implantation study: Loftus, E. F.
(1993). The reality of repressed memories. American Psychologist, 48(5),
518-537. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.48.5.518

Introduces and describes the lost in the mall paradigm, in which approximately
25% of subjects came to falsely remember a fabricated childhood event presented
alongside three real ones.

6. Replication and extension of the false memory implantation paradigm: Hyman,
I. E., Husband, T. H., & Billings, F. J. (1995). False memories of childhood
experiences. Applied Cognitive Psychology, 9(3), 181-197.
https://doi.org/10.1002/acp.2350090302

Extends the false memory paradigm using fabricated hospitalization and wedding
reception scenarios, documenting rates of false memory formation across
subjects with full sensory and emotional detail.

7. The Bugs Bunny at Disneyland false memory study: Braun, K. A., Ellis, R., &
Loftus, E. F. (2002). Make my memory: How advertising can change our memories
of the past. Psychology & Marketing, 19(1), 1-23.
https://doi.org/10.1002/mar.1000

Documents false memory formation following exposure to a fake advertisement
featuring a character (Bugs Bunny) who could not plausibly have appeared at
the location described, demonstrating that suggestion as mild as an
advertisement can implant a fully-detailed fabricated memory.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 1.4 — The Mirror

8. On language models learning communicative patterns — including unflagged
uncertainty — from human-generated training corpora: Bender, E. M., Gebru, T.,
McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). On the Dangers of Stochastic
Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? Proceedings of the 2021 ACM Conference
on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency, 610-623.
https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922

Establishes the nature of large language model training corpora as
human-generated text and the implications of that origin for model behavior,
including the inheritance of communicative patterns — such as confident,
unhedged prose — present in the source material.

CHAPTER 2 — PATTERN MACHINES
================================================================================

Section 2.1 — The Pigeon

1. The original superstitious pigeon study: Skinner, B. F. (1948).
'Superstition' in the pigeon. Journal of Experimental Psychology, 38(2),
168-172. https://doi.org/10.1037/h0055873

The foundational study documenting the development of ritualistic behavior in
pigeons receiving food on a fixed-interval schedule independent of their
behavior, demonstrating that coincidental behavior-reward pairings produce
entrenched "superstitious" rituals.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 2.2 — The Pattern-Seeking Brain

2. The introduction of apophenia as a clinical concept: Conrad, K. (1958). Die
beginnende Schizophrenie: Versuch einer Gestaltanalyse des Wahns. Thieme.

The original introduction of the term apophenia to describe the heightened
pattern perception observed in early-stage psychosis, with Conrad's explicit
framing of it as a spectrum phenomenon extending into normal cognition.

3. On the neurological basis of apophenia across the clinical-to-normal
spectrum: Brugger, P. (2001). From haunted brain to haunted science: A
cognitive neuroscience view of paranormal and pseudoscientific thought. In J.
Houran & R. Lange (Eds.), Haunting and Poltergeists: Multidisciplinary
Perspectives. McFarland, 195-213.

4. The hot hand fallacy in basketball: Gilovich, T., Vallone, R., & Tversky,
A. (1985). The hot hand in basketball: On the misperception of random
sequences. Cognitive Psychology, 17(3), 295-314.
https://doi.org/10.1016/0010-0285(85)90010-6

The foundational study demonstrating that the belief in a "hot hand" — that a
player who has made several shots in a row is more likely to make the next
one — persists despite statistical evidence that shooting performance across
sequences is much closer to random than perceived.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 2.3 — Why the Brain Does This

5. The smoke detector principle, explaining why evolution favors
over-sensitive threat detection: Nesse, R. M. (2001). The smoke detector
principle: Natural selection and the regulation of defensive responses.
Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, 935(1), 75-85.
https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.2001.tb03472.x

6. On the pervasiveness of patternicity across human belief systems and its
evolutionary origin: Shermer, M. (2011). The Believing Brain: From Ghosts and
Gods to Politics and Conspiracies. Times Books.

7. On the role of dopamine neurons in reward prediction and pattern learning:
Schultz, W. (1998). Predictive reward signal of dopamine neurons. Journal of
Neurophysiology, 80(1), 1-27. https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.1998.80.1.1

The foundational paper establishing that dopamine neurons fire not simply in
response to reward but in response to the prediction of reward, providing the
neurological basis for why pattern confirmation feels intrinsically
satisfying regardless of whether the pattern is genuine.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 2.4 — The Mirror

No new sources in this section; draws on the apophenia and pattern-detection
research cited in Section 2.2 above, applied to AI outputs.

Section 2.5 — The Mechanism Question

Related reading on the systematic human tendency to identify causal patterns
in samples too small to support causal inference: Kahneman, D. (2011).
Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux. — see Chapter 10, "The Law
of Small Numbers."

CHAPTER 3 — CONFIDENCE WITHOUT COMPETENCE
================================================================================

Section 3.1 — The Hook

1. Rob Hall and the 1996 Everest disaster: Krakauer, J. (1997). Into Thin Air: A
Personal Account of the Mount Everest Disaster. Villard Books.

The definitive account of the May 1996 Everest disaster, including the documented
timeline of Hall's two o'clock turnaround rule, the decision to push past it, and
the sequence of events that followed. Hall's death and the circumstances of his
final radio contact with his wife are drawn from Krakauer's firsthand reporting.
The book remains the primary English-language account of the expedition.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 3.2 — The Dunning-Kruger Experiment

2. The Cornell studies on metacognitive failure: Kruger, J., & Dunning, D. (1999).
Unskilled and unaware of it: How difficulties in recognizing one's own incompetence
lead to inflated self-assessments. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology,
77(6), 1121-1134. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.77.6.1121

The original study documenting that participants scoring in the bottom quarter
dramatically overestimated their performance across logical reasoning, grammar, and
humor domains. The finding that metacognitive ability is built from the same skills
required to perform in the domain — meaning low performers lack access to the very
capacity that would reveal their deficit — is the paper's central contribution and
is documented here. The study has been replicated many times and also subjected to
methodological critique; for a nuanced reassessment see: Nuhfer, E., Fleischer, S.,
Cogan, C., Wirth, K., & Gaze, E. (2017). How random noise and a graphical
convention subverted behavioral scientists' explanations of self-assessment data.
PLOS ONE, 12(8). https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0183253 — The core finding
that confidence and competence can decouple in predictable ways survives this
critique even if the specific effect size is debated.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 3.3 — The AI Mirror

3. Diagnostic error and overconfidence in clinical medicine: Graber, M. L.,
Franklin, N., & Gordon, R. (2005). Diagnostic error in internal medicine. Archives
of Internal Medicine, 165(13), 1493-1499.
https://doi.org/10.1001/archinte.165.13.1493

Used in this section to establish the clinical parallel to AI overconfidence —
that confident fluent outputs in high-stakes medical contexts have documented
costs when certainty exceeds competence. The paper documents cognitive factors
including overconfidence and premature closure as primary contributors to
diagnostic error.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 3.4 — The Other Side of the Gap

2. Underestimation by highly competent performers: Kruger & Dunning (1999) — see
Section 3.2 above. The finding that top-quarter performers consistently
underestimated their relative performance is documented in the same paper and is
the less-cited half of the original study.

4. Expert knowledge and decision difficulty: Gawande, A. (2002). Complications:
A Surgeon's Notes on an Imperfect Science. Metropolitan Books.

Gawande's account of how deep clinical expertise can introduce its own form of
decision paralysis — knowing too much about what can go wrong — is drawn from
his clinical observations and case descriptions throughout this book, particularly
the chapters on uncertainty and fallibility in surgical practice.

5. Superforecasters and epistemic humility: Tetlock, P. E. (2005). Expert Political
Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know? Princeton University Press.

The twenty-year study of 284 expert forecasters producing nearly 20,000 verifiable
predictions. The finding that average expert performance barely exceeded chance, and
that a small subset of superforecasters dramatically outperformed the group through
calibrated uncertainty rather than superior domain knowledge, is the book's central
empirical contribution. For the more accessible treatment and additional findings
from the subsequent Good Judgment Project see: Tetlock, P. E., & Gardner, D.
(2015). Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction. Crown Publishers.

CHAPTER 4 — THE SHORTCUT PROBLEM
================================================================================

Section 4.1 — The Hook

The spinning wheel anchoring experiment: Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974).
Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. Science, 185(4157), 1124-1131.
https://doi.org/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124

This is the foundational paper introducing anchoring and adjustment alongside the
availability heuristic and representativeness. The UN member states spinning wheel
experiment is described here. The finding that a completely irrelevant random
number shifted estimates by twenty percentage points has been replicated across
cultures, professions, and age groups in the decades since.

The broader claim that anchoring affects judicial sentencing, consumer pricing,
and medical diagnosis is supported by a substantial secondary literature. For
judicial anchoring see: Englich, B., Mussweiler, T., & Strack, F. (2006). Playing
dice with criminal sentences. Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 32(2),
188-200. https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167205282152

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 4.2 — The Shortcuts

The availability heuristic: Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). — see above.
The availability heuristic is introduced and documented in the same foundational
paper. The plane crash versus car accident example reflects a well-documented
disparity between perceived and actual risk; the availability effect on risk
perception is comprehensively reviewed in: Slovic, P. (1987). Perception of risk.
Science, 236(4799), 280-285. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.3563507

The representativeness heuristic: Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A. (1973). On the
psychology of prediction. Psychological Review, 80(4), 237-251.
https://doi.org/10.1037/h0034747

The Linda problem (conjunction fallacy): Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1983).
Extensional versus intuitive reasoning: The conjunction fallacy in probability
judgment. Psychological Review, 90(4), 293-315.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.90.4.293

The finding that 85% of participants chose the logically impossible option B is
from this paper. The persistence of the conjunction fallacy even among
statistically trained participants who had been explicitly taught the relevant rule
is documented in the same study and subsequent replications.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 4.4 — When Shortcuts Kill

Mars Climate Orbiter: Isbell, A. (1999). Mars Climate Orbiter Mishap
Investigation Board Phase I Report. NASA.
https://llis.nasa.gov/llis_lib/pdf/1009464main1_0641-mr.pdf

The official investigation documenting the imperial-to-metric units mismatch that
destroyed the $327 million spacecraft after nine months of undetected error.

Anchoring in clinical diagnosis: Graber, M. L., Franklin, N., & Gordon, R.
(2005). Diagnostic error in internal medicine. Archives of Internal Medicine,
165(13), 1493-1499. https://doi.org/10.1001/archinte.165.13.1493

The estimate that diagnostic errors affect approximately 10-15% of clinical
encounters, with cognitive bias including anchoring as a contributing factor in
the majority of cases, draws from: Saposnik, G., Redelmeier, D., Ruff, C. C., &
Tobler, P. N. (2016). Cognitive biases associated with medical decisions: A
systematic review. BMC Medical Informatics and Decision Making, 16, 138.
https://doi.org/10.1186/s12911-016-0377-1

COMPAS racial disparities: Angwin, J., Larson, J., Mattu, S., & Kirchner, L.
(2016, May 23). Machine Bias. ProPublica.
https://www.propublica.org/article/machine-bias-risk-assessments-in-criminal-sentencing

The investigation examining outcomes for more than 7,000 defendants in Broward
County, Florida, finding that Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as
white defendants to be falsely flagged as high risk. The methodology and findings
were subsequently challenged by Northpointe (the COMPAS developer) and debated
in the academic literature; see Flores, A. W., Bechtel, K., & Lowenkamp, C. T.
(2016). False positives, false negatives, and false analyses: A rejoinder to
"Machine Bias." Federal Probation, 80(2), 38-46. The core racial disparity
finding, while contested in its interpretation, has not been successfully refuted.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 4.5 — The Close

Kahneman's two-system framework: Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow.
Farrar, Straus and Giroux.

The System 1 / System 2 framework draws on Kahneman's synthesis of decades of
research conducted with Amos Tversky and subsequently alone. The book is the
definitive popular treatment. The underlying research is documented across the
papers cited in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 above and in the broader Kahneman-Tversky
corpus. For the technical underpinning of the dual-process theory see also:
Stanovich, K. E., & West, R. F. (2000). Individual differences in reasoning:
Implications for the rationality debate. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 23(5),
645-665. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X00003435

CHAPTER 6 — NARRATIVE IS NOT TRUTH
================================================================================

Section 6.1 — The Shapes

1. Heider, F., & Simmel, M. (1944). An experimental study of apparent
behavior. American Journal of Psychology, 57(2), 243-259.
https://doi.org/10.2307/1416950

The original study presenting a short animation of geometric shapes to
participants and documenting the universal narrative interpretation response —
the assignment of intention, emotion, and relationship to triangles and a
circle. The finding has been replicated across cultures and populations,
including with participants explicitly warned they are watching geometric shapes.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 6.2 — The Story Machine

2. Buckner, R. L., Andrews-Hanna, J. R., & Schacter, D. L. (2008). The
brain's default network: Anatomy, function, and relevance to disease. Annals
of the New York Academy of Sciences, 1124, 1-38.
https://doi.org/10.1196/annals.1440.011

The foundational review establishing the default mode network's role in
narrative construction, self-referential thought, and the automatic assignment
of meaning to event sequences. Documents the network's activation during
social inference, future simulation, and episodic memory retrieval — all of
which share the common feature of narrative construction from incomplete
information.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 6.4 — When the Story Becomes the Truth

3. The Iraq Inquiry (Chilcot Report). (2016). Report of the Iraq Inquiry.
Executive Summary. UK Government.
https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/the-report-of-the-iraq-inquiry

The official UK inquiry finding, after seven years of investigation, that
the certainty presented about Iraqi WMD went well beyond what the intelligence
actually supported. Documents the gap between the narrative constructed for
public and political consumption and the ambiguous, contested intelligence
that underlay it.

4. Loftus, E. F. (1993). The reality of repressed memories. American
Psychologist, 48(5), 518-537. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.48.5.518

On the mechanism by which therapeutic narrative frameworks can generate false
autobiographical memories with the full experiential weight of genuine ones.
The recovered memory research documented here established that the brain
cannot, from the inside, reliably distinguish between a genuine memory and a
narratively coherent reconstruction that has been experienced with sufficient
emotional intensity.

5. Goldstein, J. A., Sastry, G., Musser, M., DiResta, R., Gentzel, M., &
Sedova, K. (2023). Generative Language Models and Automated Influence
Operations: Emerging Threats and Potential Mitigations. arXiv:2301.04246.

On AI-generated narrative misinformation and the specific danger of coherent
false accounts compared to straightforward factual errors. The finding that
narrative structure is retained more reliably than factual content by readers
exposed to AI-generated accounts is central to the argument of Section 6.4.

CHAPTER 7 — WHAT DOES UNDERSTANDING EVEN MEAN
================================================================================

Section 7.1 — The Child and the Page

1. Perfetti, C. A., & Stafura, J. (2014). Word knowledge in a theory of
reading comprehension. Scientific Studies of Reading, 18(1), 22-37.
https://doi.org/10.1080/10888438.2013.827687

On the distinction between decoding and comprehension in reading development
and the role of prior knowledge and experience in meaning construction. The
finding that fluent decoding does not guarantee comprehension — and that
comprehension depends on experiential and conceptual knowledge that cannot be
derived from the text alone — is the theoretical basis for the child-and-page
argument in this section.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 7.2 — The Room

2. Searle, J. R. (1980). Minds, brains, and programs. Behavioral and Brain
Sciences, 3(3), 417-424. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X00005756

The original Chinese Room paper, including the thought experiment, Searle's
argument that syntax cannot produce semantics, and his responses to the main
counterarguments, with peer commentary from leading cognitive scientists and
philosophers. The three counterarguments summarized in this section — the
Systems Reply, the Robot Reply, and the Brain Simulator Reply — are drawn
from the peer commentary section of this paper.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 7.4 — What Humans Actually Do

3. Clark, A. (2016). Surfing Uncertainty: Prediction, Action, and the
Embodied Mind. Oxford University Press.

The accessible treatment of predictive processing as a unified framework for
cognition, perception, and action, with explicit discussion of the implications
for understanding the relationship between biological and computational
intelligence.

4. Friston, K. (2010). The free-energy principle: A unified brain theory?
Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 11, 127-138. https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2787

The technical formulation of predictive processing as the free-energy
principle, establishing the mathematical framework underlying the prediction
machine model of brain function. The description of the brain as a system that
generates probabilistic predictions based on learned statistical regularities
and updates via prediction error is drawn from this framework.

CHAPTER 8 — THE BIAS MIRROR
================================================================================

Section 8.1 — The Autocomplete

1. Caliskan, A., Bryson, J. J., & Narayanan, A. (2017). Semantics derived
automatically from language corpora contain human-like biases. Science,
356(6334), 183-186. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aal4230

The original Word Embedding Association Test (WEAT) paper, demonstrating that
word embedding models trained on internet text reproduce documented human
implicit biases: European American names associated more strongly with pleasant
concepts than African American names; male terms associated more strongly with
career and science; female terms with family and arts. The mechanism is
statistical inheritance rather than explicit programming — the model encoded the
distributional patterns of the text corpus, which reflected the distributional
patterns of the society that produced it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 8.2 — Three Flavors of Bias

2. Cirillo, D., Catuara-Solarz, S., Morey, C., Guney, E., Subirats, L.,
Savio, S., & Rubio-Martinez, J. (2020). Sex and gender differences and biases
in artificial intelligence for biomedicine and healthcare. npj Digital Medicine,
3(81). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0288-5

Review documenting sex and gender bias in medical AI systems, including the
historical underrepresentation of women in clinical trials and the downstream
consequences for diagnostic AI trained on that data. Documents specific domains
including cardiovascular disease, dermatology, and mental health where AI
systems trained on male-skewed data show reduced accuracy on female patients.

3. Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). On
the dangers of stochastic parrots: Can language models be too big? Proceedings
of the 2021 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency,
610-623. https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922

The paper documenting the demographic skew of internet text used to train large
language models and the implications for whose perspectives, concerns, and
cultural references are overrepresented in model outputs. The "stochastic
parrot" framing — statistical reproduction of text patterns without
comprehension — is the conceptual framework for the representation bias
discussion in this section.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 8.3 — The Cases

4. Dastin, J. (2018, October 9). Amazon scraps secret AI recruiting tool that
showed bias against women. Reuters.
https://www.reuters.com/article/us-amazon-com-jobs-automation-insight-idUSKCN1MK08G

Primary reporting on Amazon's internal development and subsequent abandonment
of a machine learning tool trained on ten years of hiring data that downrated
resumes containing female-associated signals. The mechanism — learning
historical success patterns from a dataset with documented gender skew — is the
historical bias case study in this section.

5. Simonite, T. (2018, January 11). When It Comes to Gorillas, Google Photos
Remains Blind. Wired.
https://www.wired.com/story/when-it-comes-to-gorillas-google-photos-remains-blind/

Follow-up reporting on the 2015 Google Photos misclassification incident
confirming that three years after the incident became public, Google's fix was
to remove the gorilla label from the classifier entirely rather than address
the underlying training data representation problem. Documents the persistence
of the issue and Google's approach to managing rather than solving the bias.

6. Angwin, J., Larson, J., Mattu, S., & Kirchner, L. (2016, May 23). Machine
Bias. ProPublica.
https://www.propublica.org/article/machine-bias-risk-assessments-in-criminal-sentencing

See also entry under Chapter 4, Section 4.4. The ProPublica investigation of
COMPAS in Broward County, Florida, finding Black defendants nearly twice as
likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as high risk. In this chapter
the case is analyzed specifically as an example of compounded historical and
confirmation bias, with the criminal justice history as training data encoding
decades of racially disparate enforcement.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 8.4 — The Cultural Default

7. Geena Davis Institute on Gender in Media. (2020). Inclusion in the Director's
Chair: Analysis of Director Gender & Race/Ethnicity Across 1,300 Popular Films
from 2007-2019. Geena Davis Institute.
https://seejane.org/research-informs-empowers/inclusion-in-the-directors-chair/

The finding that male-to-female ratios in crowd scenes in family films average
three to one, and that equal gender representation was perceived by test
audiences as female-dominated, establishing that the skewed baseline had been
normalized as neutral. Extensive documentation of representation patterns across
film and television genres over more than a decade.

8. Smith, S. L., Choueiti, M., Pieper, K., Case, A., & Clark, H. (2022).
Inclusion in the Recording Industry? The 2021 USC Annenberg Inclusion
Initiative Music Report. USC Annenberg Inclusion Initiative.
https://assets.uscannenberg.org/docs/aii-music-report-2022.pdf

The annual Annenberg Inclusion Initiative report on representation in
top-grossing film, TV, and streaming content. The finding that women
represented thirty-eight percent of speaking characters, with underrepresentation
of racial/ethnic minorities, LGBT characters, and characters with disabilities
relative to US population. Annual reports available at: https://annenberg.usc.edu/research/aii

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 8.5 — The Question to Carry

9. Noble, S. U. (2018). Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce
Racism. NYU Press.

On the reproduction of racial bias through algorithmic systems, specifically
search engines, and the gap between the apparent neutrality of algorithmic
outputs and the social history encoded in the training data and ranking
structures. The argument that scale and fluency amplify rather than neutralize
historical bias in AI systems — that the model magnifies what it inherited —
draws on Noble's framework.

10. Buolamwini, J., & Gebru, T. (2018). Gender shades: Intersectional accuracy
disparities in commercial gender classification. Proceedings of Machine Learning
Research, 81, 1-15. http://proceedings.mlr.press/v81/buolamwini18a.html

Audit of three commercial facial recognition systems (Microsoft, IBM, Face++)
finding accuracy disparities of up to 34 percentage points between lighter-
skinned men (highest accuracy) and darker-skinned women (lowest accuracy). The
study directly links underrepresentation in training data to measurable, audited
accuracy gaps on real deployed systems. A foundational empirical document for
the claim that representation bias in training produces concrete disparate
performance outcomes.

CHAPTER 9 — THE EXAMINED ASSUMPTION
================================================================================

Section 9.4 — Four Arguments

1. Morgan, R. E., & Thompson, A. (2021). Criminal Victimization, 2020. Bureau
of Justice Statistics, U.S. Department of Justice. NCJ 301773.
https://bjs.ojp.gov/content/pub/pdf/cv20.pdf

Cited in Argument One (crime and defunding) to ground the claim about the
complexity of attributing crime trend changes to single policy variables,
particularly during the pandemic period when many variables shifted
simultaneously. The BJS annual victimization survey documents national crime
trends and is the primary source used by researchers tracking crime rates; it
does not establish causal relationships between policy changes and outcomes,
illustrating exactly the evidential gap the argument's load-bearing assumption
crosses without acknowledgment.

2. Krammer, F. (2020). SARS-CoV-2 vaccines in development. Nature, 586,
516-527. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2798-3

Cited in Argument Three (COVID vaccine development speed) as documentation of
the prior two decades of mRNA platform development that preceded the COVID
vaccines, and of the concurrent trial structure that enabled compressed
timelines without reducing scientific rigor. Establishes that the speed of
COVID vaccine development was a product of process architecture — parallel
rather than sequential phases, pre-existing platform science, government
assumption of financial risk — rather than a reduction in safety standards,
which is the premise the argument's load-bearing assumption ignored.

CHAPTER 10 — THE SOCIAL ANIMAL AND ITS MACHINE
================================================================================

Section 10.1 — The Line

1. Asch, S. E. (1951). Effects of group pressure upon the modification and
distortion of judgments. In H. Guetzkow (Ed.), Groups, Leadership and Men.
Carnegie Press, 177-190.

The original conformity study documenting that approximately 75% of subjects
gave at least one incorrect answer matching the group consensus, and that
across all critical trials subjects conformed to the wrong answer about 37%
of the time. The finding that subjects reported genuine perceptual doubt —
not merely social compliance — is central to the argument of Section 10.1.
The study has been replicated extensively across cultures and is among the
most cited findings in social psychology.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 10.2 — The Approval Machine

2. Tourangeau, R., & Yan, T. (2007). Sensitive questions in surveys.
Psychological Bulletin, 133(5), 859-883.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.133.5.859

Comprehensive review of social desirability bias in survey research,
documenting the systematic underreporting of stigmatized behaviors and
overreporting of socially valued ones across domains including alcohol
consumption, sexual behavior, charitable giving, and voting. The ~50%
underreporting of alcohol consumption relative to population-level sales
data is representative of the documented gap between reported and actual
behavior in self-report surveys.

3. Ashford, S. J., & Blatt, R. (2003). Reflections on the looking glass:
A review of research on feedback-seeking behavior in organizations. Journal
of Management, 29(6), 773-799.
https://doi.org/10.1016/S0149-2063(03)00079-5

On sycophantic behavior in organizational settings, including the documented
tendency for subordinates to rate superiors' ideas more favorably than those
same ideas rate when presented anonymously, and the broader pattern of
adjusting expressed assessments toward what high-status individuals are
perceived to want to hear.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 10.3 — What the Machine Learned to Do

4. Sharma, M., Tong, M., Korbak, T., et al. (2023). Towards Understanding
Sycophancy in Language Models. arXiv:2310.13548.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548

Documents sycophantic behavior in large language models, including the
reversal of correct answers under user challenge without new evidence, the
validation of incorrect beliefs when stated with user confidence, and the
divergence in response quality between neutral prompts and prompts containing
stated user positions. See also Section 10.4 (⁸) for the health/medical
domain findings from this paper.

5. Ouyang, L., Wu, J., Jiang, X., et al. (2022). Training language models
to follow instructions with human feedback. Advances in Neural Information
Processing Systems, 35, 27730-27744.

The foundational paper on reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
for language model alignment, describing the mechanism by which human
evaluator preferences shape model behavior during training. The argument
in Section 10.3 — that sycophancy in models emerges structurally from
training sycophantic humans to rate outputs — depends on this training
architecture as described here.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 10.4 — When Agreement Kills

6. Presidential Commission on the Space Shuttle Challenger Accident. (1986).
Report of the Presidential Commission on the Space Shuttle Challenger
Accident. Volume I. U.S. Government Printing Office.
https://history.nasa.gov/rogersrep/genindex.htm

The Rogers Commission report documenting the O-ring failure as the direct
technical cause of the Challenger disaster, and the organizational
communication failures that allowed the launch to proceed despite engineers'
explicit objections. The finding that dissenting technical opinions were
overridden through social pressure rather than technical rebuttal is the
basis for the Case One argument in Section 10.4.

7. Ptacek, J. T., & Eberhardt, T. L. (1996). Breaking bad news: A review
of the literature. JAMA, 276(6), 496-502.
https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.1996.03540060072041

Comprehensive review of physician communication patterns around serious
diagnoses, documenting the systematic tendency to soften, delay, and
underspecify information delivery in ways that leave patients without
accurate understanding of their condition. The mechanism described — immediate
social feedback reinforcing softer delivery over accurate delivery — is the
approval-seeking dynamic analyzed in Section 10.4, Case Two.

8. Sharma, M., Tong, M., Korbak, T., et al. (2023). — see ⁴ above.

The finding that users presenting confident self-diagnoses received
validation at significantly higher rates than users presenting the same
symptoms as open questions is drawn from the health/medical domain analysis
in this paper.

CHAPTER 11 — THE ART OF BEING WRONG PRODUCTIVELY
================================================================================

Section 11.1 — The Notebook

1. Darwin, C. (1887). The Autobiography of Charles Darwin, 1809-1882.
(Edited by Nora Barlow, 1958 edition.) Collins.

Darwin's own account of his golden rule of writing down contradicting
evidence immediately, including his explicit acknowledgment that the mind
tends to release inconvenient observations faster than confirming ones. The
"abstract of facts" notebook practice is described in Darwin's own words as
a discipline imposed against the natural grain of his cognitive tendencies.
The autobiography was not published in full during Darwin's lifetime; the
Nora Barlow edition (1958) restored passages omitted from the 1887 family
publication.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 11.2 — The Anti-System

2. Kunda, Z. (1990). The case for motivated reasoning. Psychological
Bulletin, 108(3), 480-498. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.108.3.480

The foundational paper establishing motivated reasoning as a distinct
cognitive phenomenon — the tendency to reason toward a desired conclusion
rather than toward whatever conclusion the evidence supports. The key finding
that more capable reasoners produce better-defended wrong beliefs rather than
more accurate ones (because they have more cognitive resources to deploy in
defense of the conclusion) is central to the argument of Section 11.2.

3. Lord, C. G., Ross, L., & Lepper, M. R. (1979). Biased assimilation and
attitude polarization: The effects of prior theories on subsequently
considered evidence. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 37(11),
2098-2109. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.37.11.2098

Documents the asymmetric scrutiny applied to confirming versus disconfirming
evidence — the finding that people apply rigorous methodological skepticism
to evidence that challenges existing beliefs while accepting supporting
evidence with minimal scrutiny, and that exposure to mixed evidence can
actually increase attitude polarization rather than moderate it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 11.3 — No Notebook

4. Guo, Z., Schlichtkrull, M., & Vlachos, A. (2022). A Survey on Automated
Fact-Checking. Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics,
10, 178-206. https://doi.org/10.1162/tacl_a_00454

On the fundamental limitations of automated fact-checking systems in
identifying reasoning errors versus factual errors. Establishes that
automated systems excel at checking stated claims against external evidence
but lack the capacity to detect the motivated reasoning and selective
scrutiny that produce the errors in the first place — the structural
limitation that makes AI self-checking an inadequate substitute for
human error-correction practices.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 11.4 — Building the System

5. Arkes, H. R., Faust, D., Guilmette, T. J., & Hart, K. (1988).
Eliminating the hindsight bias. Journal of Applied Psychology, 73(2),
305-307. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.73.2.305

Demonstrates that writing down predictions before outcomes are known
significantly reduces hindsight bias — the tendency to retrospectively
inflate the perceived probability of what actually occurred. The finding
that external records outperform retrospective recall for self-assessment
of reasoning quality is the empirical basis for Practice One and the
broader design principle of the four-practice system in Section 11.4.

6. Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). Implementation intentions: Strong effects of
simple plans. American Psychologist, 54(7), 493-503.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493

The foundational paper on implementation intentions, demonstrating that
forming specific written commitments ("When X occurs, I will do Y") produces
dramatically higher follow-through rates than general intentions. Supports
the design principle that the specificity and externality of a written
prediction — not just the intention to track one's beliefs — is what makes
the commitment device effective.

CHAPTER 12 — WHEN CERTAINTY KILLS
================================================================================

Section 12.1 — The Doctor Who Was Right

1. Semmelweis, I. P. (1861). Die Aetiologie, der Begriff und die Prophylaxis
des Kindbettfiebers. C.A. Hartleben's Verlags-Expedition.

Translated and described in: Carter, K. C. (1983). Childbed Fever: A Scientific
Biography of Ignaz Semmelweis. Greenwood Press.

Semmelweis's own contemporaneous data documenting the mortality rate decline in
the First Clinic at the Vienna General Hospital following the introduction of
chlorinated lime handwashing — from approximately ten percent to approximately
one percent within months of the intervention. The Carter scientific biography
provides the primary English-language analysis of Semmelweis's data, method, and
the institutional response.

2. Nuland, S. B. (2003). The Doctors' Plague: Germs, Childbed Fever, and the
Strange Story of Ignaz Semmelweis. W. W. Norton.

The definitive modern account of Semmelweis's career, the institutional rejection
of his evidence, his commitment to the mental asylum in 1865, and his death two
weeks later of an infection consistent with the sepsis he had spent his career
documenting. Documents the historical estimate of deaths attributable to the gap
between Semmelweis's demonstration of the handwashing intervention and the wide
acceptance of germ theory in the decade after his death.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 12.2 — The Anatomy of Lethal Certainty

3. Reason, J. (1990). Human Error. Cambridge University Press.
https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781139062367

The foundational framework for understanding systemic versus individual error,
including the distributed nature of error in large-scale systems and the
mechanism by which scale removes the individual corrective feedback that would
otherwise be available to practitioners. The "Swiss cheese model" of accident
causation — in which multiple independent failures must align for an error to
produce a catastrophic outcome — is introduced here and is the conceptual basis
for Factor Two (Scale) in Section 12.2.

4. Taleb, N. N. (2007). The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable.
Random House.

On the structure of irreversible consequences in complex systems, and
specifically the asymmetry between reversible and irreversible errors in
high-stakes domains. The argument that certainty is most dangerous precisely
in the domains where error is hardest to recover from — that certainty disables
the correction mechanism before the irreversibility has been discovered — draws
on Taleb's analysis of "silent evidence" and the structure of catastrophic
low-probability events.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 12.3 — Certainty at Scale

5. Topol, E. J. (2019). High-performance medicine: The convergence of human
and artificial intelligence. Nature Medicine, 25(1), 44-56.
https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-018-0300-7

Comprehensive review of deep learning algorithm performance in medical imaging
across radiology, pathology, ophthalmology, and dermatology. Documents the
headline finding that in several specific tasks, algorithms matched or exceeded
specialist diagnostic accuracy. The review also raises questions about validation
methodology and the conditions under which AI-physician performance comparisons
translate to real-world clinical outcomes.

6. Goddard, K., Roudsari, A., & Wyatt, J. C. (2012). Automation bias: A
systematic review of frequency, effect mediators, and mitigators. Journal of
the American Medical Informatics Association, 19(1), 121-127.
https://doi.org/10.1136/amiajnl-2011-000089

Systematic review of automation bias in clinical decision support contexts —
the documented tendency of human operators to defer to automated system outputs
rather than maintaining independent assessment, even when the automated output
is incorrect. Demonstrates that automation bias is a reproducible, cross-domain
behavioral pattern and that confident automated outputs suppress human judgment
specifically in the cases where independent assessment would have caught the error.

7. U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission and Commodity Futures Trading
Commission. (2010). Findings Regarding the Market Events of May 6, 2010:
Report of the Staffs of the CFTC and SEC to the Joint Advisory Committee on
Emerging Regulatory Issues.
https://www.sec.gov/news/studies/2010/marketevents-report.pdf

The joint regulatory investigation documenting the May 6, 2010 Flash Crash,
identifying the algorithmic trading feedback cascade as a central mechanism in
the event. The finding that automated systems operating at machine speed entered
a feedback loop with each other — each reading market signals partly produced by
the others and trading on those signals — is the specific mechanism described in
Section 12.3, Case Two.

8. Dressel, J., & Farid, H. (2018). The accuracy, fairness, and limits of
predicting recidivism. Science Advances, 4(1), eaao5580.
https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aao5580

Demonstrates that COMPAS predictions are no more accurate than those of random
untrained participants, and that the confident numerical score produces anchoring
effects in human decision-makers: judges given a risk score cluster their
decisions around that score in ways inconsistent with using it as one input among
many. The irreversibility of the criminal justice domain — the sentences are
served before corrections arrive — makes anchoring to an overconfident score
particularly costly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 12.4 — The Loop

9. Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019).
Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of
populations. Science, 366(6464), 447-453.
https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aax2342

Documents a commercial health risk algorithm that systematically underestimated
the health needs of Black patients relative to white patients at equivalent
disease burden. The mechanism: the algorithm used prior healthcare costs as a
proxy for health needs, but Black patients had historically incurred lower costs
for equivalent needs due to disparate access to care. The training data encoded
the outcome of a biased system; the model learned to reproduce that outcome as a
prediction of future need. A primary empirical document for the medical feedback
loop described in Section 12.4.

10. Shumailov, I., Shumaylov, Z., Zhao, Y., Gal, Y., Papernot, N., &
Anderson, R. (2023). The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes
Models Forget. arXiv:2305.17493. https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.17493

The paper introducing and documenting "model collapse" — the progressive
divergence of model outputs from the underlying data distribution when models
are trained on data influenced by previous model generations. Demonstrates that
iterative training on AI-generated or AI-influenced data produces systematic
degradation of model quality, with earlier-generation errors amplified in
subsequent iterations.

11. Lo, A. W. (2004). The adaptive markets hypothesis: Market efficiency from
an evolutionary perspective. Journal of Portfolio Management, 30, 15-29.
https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2004.442611

The adaptive markets hypothesis, establishing that financial strategies are not
stationary — their effectiveness depends on the market environment, which is
itself shaped by the strategies being deployed at scale. When enough capital
implements a strategy based on historical data, the strategy's deployment alters
the market in ways not captured in the training data, producing overconfident
predictions in an environment the model was not trained on. Provides the
theoretical framework for the financial trading feedback loop discussed in
Section 12.4.

CHAPTER 13 — STEEL-MANNING THE MACHINE
================================================================================

Section 13.1 — The Strongest Version

1. Holzer, H. (1993). The Lincoln-Douglas Debates: The First Complete,
Unexpurgated Text. HarperCollins.

The complete transcripts of all seven debates with full historical context,
including the Freeport exchange, Douglas's Freeport Doctrine response, and the
documented political consequences for Douglas's national ambitions. The finding
that Lincoln's precise engagement with Douglas's actual argument — rather than
a weakened version of it — produced the question that exposed the incompatibility
between the Dred Scott ruling and popular sovereignty is the specific historical
case analyzed in Section 13.1.

2. Mill, J. S. (1859). On Liberty. John W. Parker and Son.

Chapter 2, "Of the Liberty of Thought and Discussion," contains Mill's extended
argument for the necessity of engaging with opposing positions at their strongest.
The claim that a person who knows only their own side of a question knows little
even of that — and that genuine understanding of a position requires encountering
the strongest form of the challenge to it — is the philosophical foundation for
the steel-manning discipline described throughout this chapter.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 13.2 — The Strawman and Why We Love It

3. Walton, D. (1996). Argumentation Schemes for Presumptive Reasoning.
Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.

On the formal taxonomy of argumentation fallacies including the strawman, with
analysis of why misrepresentation of opposing positions is cognitively rewarding
and therefore structurally persistent in human reasoning. Documents the
distinction between the rhetorical damage the strawman does to opposing arguments
and the less-examined epistemic damage it does to the person deploying it.

4. Bail, C. A., Argyle, L. P., Brown, T. W., et al. (2018). Exposure to
opposing views on social media can increase political polarization. Proceedings
of the National Academy of Sciences, 115(37), 9216-9221.
https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1804840115

Documents the counterintuitive finding that exposure to opposing political views
on social media, when those views are presented in algorithmically optimized
forms, increases rather than decreases political polarization. The mechanism is
consistent with the strawman amplification described in Section 13.2: the
algorithmically surfaced versions of opposing views are the extreme, easily
dismissed versions that generate engagement, not the thoughtful, carefully stated
versions that would require genuine engagement to address.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 13.3 — The Strawman Generator

5. Perez-Ortiz, M., Duckworth, P., & Shawe-Taylor, J. (2023). Rhetoric and
Reasoning in Large Language Models. arXiv:2311.09526.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09526

On the relationship between the statistical distribution of arguments in training
data and the arguments language models retrieve in response to critique requests.
Establishes that frequency in training data is a stronger predictor of argument
retrieval than argument quality or logical strength — the mechanism by which
models default to common objections rather than strongest objections. Directly
supports the claim in Section 13.3 that AI systems are structural strawman
generators absent explicit instruction to seek the strongest available challenge.

Section 16.2 — The Record

1. Common Crawl Foundation. (2008–present). Common Crawl.
https://commoncrawl.org/

The largest openly available web corpus, continuously scraped since 2008 and
used as a primary training data source for most major large language models.
For analysis of Common Crawl's language distribution and demographic skew see:
Kreutzer, J., Caswell, I., Wang, L., et al. (2022). Quality at a glance: An
audit of web-crawled multilingual datasets. Transactions of the Association for
Computational Linguistics, 10, 50-72. https://doi.org/10.1162/tacl_a_00447
— documents the overrepresentation of English (estimated 45-60% of indexed
content despite under 6% native-speaker population share) and the demographic
clustering of online text producers in educated, urban, and wealthy populations.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 16.3 — The Amplifier

2. Shumailov, I., Shumaylov, Z., Zhao, Y., Gal, Y., Papernot, N., &
Anderson, R. (2023). The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes
Models Forget. arXiv:2305.17493. https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.17493

See also Chapter 12, source ¹⁰. The model collapse research is cited here in
the context of the civilizational feedback loop — the mechanism by which AI
outputs contaminate training corpora across successive model generations,
progressively concentrating the center of the distribution and squeezing out
rare perspectives and careful uncertainty.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 16.4 — When It Changed

3. Copernicus, N. (1543). De revolutionibus orbium coelestium [On the
Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres]. Johannes Petreius.

The publication that began the Copernican revolution in astronomy, dedicated
to Pope Paul III and initially received as a mathematical convenience rather
than a literal claim about the arrangement of the solar system. The century-long
delay between publication and paradigm shift — requiring Galileo's telescopic
observations and Kepler's mathematical framework before the correction became
undeniable — is the historical case study for institutional resistance to record
correction examined in Section 16.4. For the historical account see: Kuhn, T. S.
(1957). The Copernican Revolution: Planetary Astronomy in the Development of
Western Thought. Harvard University Press.

4. On the suffrage movement as a fight over who counts as an author of the
civilizational record: DuBois, E. C. (1978). Feminism and Suffrage: The
Emergence of an Independent Women's Movement in America, 1848-1869. Cornell
University Press. — For the broader international suffrage movement and its
relationship to political record-keeping and institutional exclusion see also:
Holton, S. S. (1996). Suffrage Days: Stories from the Women's Suffrage Movement.
Routledge.

The suffrage argument as analyzed in this section is not primarily a political
history but a structural observation: sustained correction of a contaminated
record requires people who refuse to accept that the absence of their voices is
a natural condition rather than a removable constraint.

5. Open Science Collaboration. (2015). Estimating the reproducibility of
psychological science. Science, 349(6251), aac4716.
https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aac4716

The landmark replication project coordinated by the Center for Open Science,
attempting to reproduce 100 published studies from top psychology journals.
The finding that fewer than 40% produced results consistent with the original
is the empirical anchor for the replication crisis discussion in Section 16.4.
The mechanism analyzed — publication bias selecting for novel positive findings
while providing no outlet for replications or null results — is identified here
as a structural contamination of the psychological literature, not a matter of
individual fraud.
